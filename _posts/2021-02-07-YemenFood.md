On Friday, the State Department announced that it would revoke Trump's 11th-hour designation of the Yemeni Ansar Allah movement (aka the Houthis) as a Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO). While the decision to revoke the designation was not as clear-cut as some argued, I believe this was the right call. The Houthis are as oppressive and kleptocratic as they come, but the reality is that these FTO sanctions would have exacerbated the humanitarian crisis in the country. And, moreover, the sanctions would have had little effect on the Houthi leadership, given the movement's control over the country's black market economy (i.e. most of the economy).

I created the above map to show why the sanctions would have been devastating for the poorest Yemenis. The Famine Early Warning System Network (or FEWS NET) is a research consortium that tracks food insecurity through field research and data collection. According to FEWS NET nearly all Yemenis are currently living in areas experiencing a food security "crisis." This means that households must "deplete essential livelihood assets" to meet their dietary needs. Basically people are selling their belongings just to pay for their next meal. The situation is bad all over, but the worth insecurity is found in the northwest, which is also the most populous region. This area mostly falls in Houthi territory. In these areas, FEWS NET predicts that if current humanitarian programming were to end, the situation would be drastically worse. And this is why the FTO designation would have been so damaging. Humanitarians would need to jump through additional hoops to operate in the area, depriving Yemenis of aid. More importantly, international commercial traders would begin to shy away from doing business in Yemen for fear of sanctions-related litigation, leading food prices to sky-rocket (humanitarians reported early signs of this despite Biden's previous pause on the designation). 

The calculations included in the map are not straight forward to calculate because the vast majority of Yemenis are already food insecure. Also FEWS NET classifications are qualitative in nature, although they are developed by food security experts and based on survey and other data. Just because a location is classified as an "emergency" does not mean that everyone living in that area is even food insecure. There are class differences in Yemen as elsewhere. Despite the limitations, the FEWS NET and WorldPop data are useful resource for understanding a truly awful situation in Yemen.
