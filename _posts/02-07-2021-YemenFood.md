On Friday, the State Department announced that it planned to revoke Trump's 11th-hour designation of the Yemeni Ansar Allah movement (aka the Houthis) as a Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO). While the decision to revoke the designationwas not as clear-cut as some have said, I believe this was the right call. The Houthis are as oppressive and kleptocratic as they come, but the reality is that these FTO sanctions would have likely pushed millions of Yemenis into a famine. And, given the Houthi's grip over the blackmarket economy (i.e. the economy), the sanctions likely would not have had much bite for them anyway. 

These sanctions would have been devastating for Yemen's poor, who primarily live in areas controlled by the Houthis. I created the above map to illustrate this point. According to the Famine Early Warning System Network (or FEWS NET), the most food-insecure areas of Yemen are the Houthi-controlled northwest (home to around 15 million people). These areas are classified as living through a food security "crisis". This means that "households either have food consumption gaps that are reflected by high or above-usual acute malnutrition; or are marginally able to meet minimum food needs but only by depleting essential livelihood assets or through crisis-coping strategies."

According to FEWS NET, without the current humanitarian programming in the northwest (i.e. the aid threatened by this FTO designation), these areas would slip into a food insecurity "emergency", one level below famine. 

The above calculations were not straight forward to calculate because the vast majority of Yemenis are already food insecure. Around 28 million people (out of a population of 29 million ) are living in areas classified as experiencing a "crisis." Also FEWS NET classifications are qualitative in nature, although they are developed by food security experts and based on survey and other data. Just because a location is classified as an "emergency" does not mean that everyone living in that area is even food insecure. There are class differences in Yemen as elsewhere. Despite the limitations, the FEWS NET and WorldPop data are useful resource for understanding a truly awful situation in Yemen.
